Using ChatGPT for this assignment was very helpful and fun, because I have never
built a Swing GUI this polished before, and by reading through ChatGPT’s code,
I learned a bunch more uses and functionalities of this interface.
It handled the boilerplate, the panel structure, and the event wiring for
DEAL, DRAW, MAX BET, and RESET.
I also found ChatGPT’s suggestions very useful, like using JToggleButtons
for the cards, with a yellow border and “HOLD” labels to show which cards
the player keeps. That gave me a much nicer front end than I would have
written on my own in the same amount of time.

At the same time, this homework made it very clear that I still had to
understand the code and reason about bugs myself.
For example, at one point the cards looked like they were covered in a
white sheet after drawing; this turned out to be a Swing issue with
disabled buttons and transparency, not a poker logic bug.
I had to interpret what was happening myself by reading through the code
and doing research, describe it to ChatGPT, and then selectively modify
the generated code (for example, leaving the card buttons enabled and
relying on my own handDealt flag).
I also had to plug in my own hand-evaluation method (Game.checkHand)
and verify that the payouts understood the fundamental mechanism
behind the game, because I wrote the back end.

Overall, I learned that ChatGPT is great for generating GUIs and giving
me a starting point, but I am still responsible for debugging,
integrating with my own logic, and making sure the final program behaves
correctly, as well as understanding what ChatGPT writes to a certain
extent.
I thought this was a great exercise and very useful in real-world
scenarios.
As Jensen Huang said in an interview, programmers who learn to  
collaborate with AI will outperform those who don’t and this assignment
made me appreciate what that means.
